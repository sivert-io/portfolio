Game development is where I built most of my technical foundation. I’ve been working with game engines since around 2015, starting in Unity, where I learned C#, object-oriented programming, and how to structure larger interactive systems.

After about five years with Unity, I explored Unreal Engine and its Blueprint scripting model. While it was interesting to work with, it never felt like the right fit for how I like to build software, so I continued with Unity until around 2022, when I picked up Godot. Since then, Godot has become my preferred engine thanks to its flexibility, workflow, and programming model.

Over the years, game development has given me experience far beyond just making games. It taught me how to think in systems, work with event-driven logic, structure reusable code, debug complex interactions, and iterate quickly on both technical and user-facing problems. It was also one of the main ways I developed confidence in programming as a whole.

I’ve also spent time building on Roblox on and off, where I created a few games that performed well on the platform. That gave me practical experience not just in gameplay programming, but also in building for real players, shipping quickly, and learning from how people actually interact with what you make.

Game development has been a long-running thread throughout my career, and it has shaped a lot of how I think about software engineering today.

**Tech:** Unity, C#, Godot, GDScript, Unreal Engine, Blueprint scripting, Roblox, gameplay systems, object-oriented programming, rapid prototyping
